## Prompt
Ok, let’s extend the experiment. The additional problem to solve is getting employers of the workers to pay for the workers healthcare. Because these workers are usually low-skilled agriculture workers or similar low-skilled workers - some of them undocumented - employers don’t want to pay for benefits, much less insurance for healthcare. So, solve for the problem of how to get employers to pay for their healthcare. It’s also important to understand that employers have all the leverage while their workers have almost none; especially if they’re undocumented and afraid of being deported.

# Expert Disagreement Breakdown
The key correction from the model: employer consent is not the binding constraint. The binding constraint is political feasibility — whether the state can pass and enforce rules despite employer lobbying.

Biggest expert disagreements
A. Feasibility
Some experts say employer mandates are unrealistic because agriculture has thin margins, seasonal labor, and subcontracting.
Others argue pooled insurance, public options, or state-managed funds can make mandates workable.

B. Legality
Disagreement over whether Washington can mandate healthcare contributions for undocumented workers.
Main concerns: federal preemption, immigration law conflicts, and employer classification.

C. Enforcement
Some experts worry mandates will push employers underground, increase misclassification, or make workers more vulnerable.
Others argue strong labor enforcement, anonymous reporting, and licensing/subsidy hooks can make compliance possible.

D. Cost incidence
Debate over who really pays: employers, workers through lower wages, consumers through food prices, or taxpayers through subsidies.
This matters politically because opponents will frame it as a farm-killer.

E. Political strategy
Incrementalists favor voluntary programs, subsidies, pilots, and public clinics first.
Mandate advocates argue voluntary programs fail because employers have all the leverage.

Why employer agreement is the wrong target
The model’s bottom line: do not design the program around employer goodwill.

Employers will likely oppose it. The strategy has to be:
bypass employer consent,
use state authority,
create enforceable obligations,
protect workers from retaliation, (1/3)
build a political coalition strong enough to survive lobbying.

Strongest state levers
The most credible levers are:

Employer healthcare contribution fee
Applied to agricultural employers.
Funds a state-managed rural worker healthcare pool.

Licensing/subsidy hooks
Tie eligibility for state agricultural subsidies, water rights support, labor programs, or licenses to participation.

Public option / state-subsidized insurance pool
Coverage exists even if individual employers resist.

Worker protection enforcement
Anonymous reporting.
Retaliation penalties.
Legal aid.
Immigration-safe access channels.

Mobile/rural clinics
State-funded care delivery for workers who cannot safely rely on employer-provided systems.

Anti-lobbying coalition strategy

farmworker advocacy groups,

Strategy:

Evidence that would change expert minds
Experts would likely shift if Washington could show:
pilot programs improved health outcomes,
employer mandates did not destroy farm viability,
worker retaliation was low,
undocumented workers actually used the system,
legal risks were manageable,
voters supported rural/agricultural worker healthcare access.

Bottom line
The best policy path is not employer agreement.

It is a state-backed, enforceable employer-funded rural healthcare pool with:
strong anti-retaliation protections,
immigration-safe access,
public clinics/mobile care,
phased mandates,
subsidies or fee scaling, (2/3)
and a political coalition built before the mandate fight.

The core disagreement is not whether employers will resist — they will. The disagreement is whether the state has enough political will and enforcement capacity to make employer funding happen anyway. (3/3)
