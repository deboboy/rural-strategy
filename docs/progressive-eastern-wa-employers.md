The strongest Eastern/Central Washington ag employers that look like “outlier” worker-care models are:

Stemilt Growers — Wenatchee
Most mature model: full Equitable Food Initiative (EFI) certification across company-owned orchards, packing, and warehouses; worker-manager teams; onsite clinic/pharmacy; ethical H-2A recruitment through Cierto; high return rates; measurable safety/productivity gains. Caveat: Stemilt has prior H-2A litigation/settlement history, so it is a strong current system but not an unblemished record.

Rainier Fruit / Zirkle Fruit — Selah / Yakima Valley / Mattawa
Early Washington tree-fruit adopter of EFI; formalized “grow people as well as fruit” culture into worker-manager leadership teams, anti-retaliation protections, food-safety/worker-voice systems, and pandemic-era safety processes.

FirstFruits Farms — Prescott, WA
100% EFI-certified farming operation; explicitly centers worker voice and “good people grow great fruit.” One of its workers, Maria de Sagrario Iglesias, was named EFI’s inaugural Farmworker of the Year after 35 years with the company.

CMI Orchards / McDougall & Sons — Central Washington grower-owned network
EFI certification across a large share of its apple/cherry/pear acreage; uses worker-manager leadership teams, training, safer work environments, and continuous improvement. Scalable because it is embedded in a grower-owned marketing/supply structure.

Stormy Mountain Labor Association — Wenatchee
Not a classic employer, but important: it is building a centralized H-2A pathway for Pacific Northwest fruit growers, with a $1.2M USDA grant. This is scalable because it shifts worker recruitment/support from isolated farm-by-farm improvisation to a shared regional labor infrastructure.

DNS Farm Services / Dovex Fruit — Wenatchee   (1/8)
Contested but worth watching. USDA awarded it $720K for H-2A workforce reliability, safety/health, overtime support, and Fair Food Program participation. But Dovex has past labor/worker-rights litigation, so I would treat it as an emerging/turnaround case, not a clean exemplar.

The scalable thing they have built is not charity. It is a worker-retention and risk-management operating system: certification, worker voice, ethical recruitment, legal migration pathways, safety systems, benefits/housing infrastructure, and buyer-facing proof that workers are being treated better.

Why these employers can be progressive in a hostile federal climate
The current federal direction appears to be moving against farmworker protections:

The Trump administration’s 2025 H-2A wage changes are described by EPI and state attorneys general as lowering wages for H-2A workers, including through a two-tier wage structure and housing-cost deductions.
The Department of Labor also moved to rescind parts of the 2024 H-2A worker-protection rule.
Washington agriculture meanwhile faces labor shortages, deportation fears, and rising H-2A costs.

So these Eastern Washington employers are outliers because they are not waiting for the federal floor to rise. They are building above-compliance systems for practical reasons:

Labor scarcity makes retention existential.
If you cannot keep workers, you cannot harvest fruit.

Worker voice improves operations.
EFI’s model turns workers into problem-spotters: safety, ergonomics, water access, traffic lanes, ladder risks, packhouse flow, food safety.

Certification creates market leverage.
Retailers and food buyers increasingly want proof of responsible labor practices.

Legal/ethical recruitment reduces chaos.
Cierto, H-2A pathways, and centralized labor associations reduce recruiter abuse, debt, abandonment, and turnover.

Costs are shared.   (2/8)
Group health plans, labor associations, USDA grants, and public-private housing funding make worker care more affordable than if each farm acts alone.

Washington state law gives them a higher baseline than federal law.
State wage, heat, housing, and labor rules matter more locally than federal retrenchment.

Stemilt Growers — Wenatchee, WA
Why it stands out: Stemilt has one of the most complete worker-care systems in Washington tree fruit.

Documented pieces:

Full EFI certification across company-owned orchards, packing facilities, and warehouses.
Meets 300+ EFI standards across fields and packing facilities.
Uses worker-manager collaboration and continuous-improvement teams.
Reports:
15% reduction in workplace injuries from 2023 to 2024.
16% productivity increase per employee since 2021.
50% reduction in handling damage within one year.
9.3-year average tenure for year-round employees.
96% H-2A return rate.
80% local seasonal return rate.
More than $800,000 in EFI premium bonuses paid to workers.
Offers an onsite health clinic and pharmacy for employees and families.
Partners with Cierto Global for transparent, worker-centered H-2A recruitment.
Stemilt says all H-2A workers go through Cierto’s “Plan de Vida” training, which includes financial planning and life planning.

Scalable model:
Stemilt shows that worker care can be converted into a repeatable management system: certification + worker-manager teams + ethical recruitment + safety redesign + retention metrics + health access.

Important caveat:   (3/8)
Stemilt had a major H-2A class-action case, Gomez v. Stemilt Ag Services, involving allegations from 2017 across Eastern Washington orchards. A $3M settlement was approved in 2021; Stemilt did not admit wrongdoing. So the fair read is: strong current system, but not a spotless historical record.

The scalable patterns to copy
If the question is “what have they built that’s scalable,” the answer is these five models:

Worker-manager leadership teams
 (7/8)
Used by EFI-certified employers. Workers and managers jointly identify problems and redesign work. This turns worker care into operational improvement, not just moral messaging.

Third-party certification
EFI and Fair Food Program give buyers, retailers, and workers a common standard. This makes worker care auditable and market-relevant.

Ethical/legal recruitment pipelines
Cierto, H-2A pathways, and labor associations reduce exploitative recruitment fees, contract abandonment, and undocumented labor dependence.

Shared infrastructure
Health benefits, housing, recruitment, and worker support become cheaper when shared across employers through associations, co-ops, or regional labor groups.

Retention economics
High return rates, long tenure, fewer injuries, better productivity, and lower turnover are the business case. In a tight labor market, taking care of workers is not anti-business; it is how the business survives.

Bottom-line recommendation
For a deeper story, I would focus on Stemilt, Rainier/Zirkle, FirstFruits, and CMI/McDougall & Sons as the core Eastern Washington exemplars. Then use Stormy Mountain Labor Association and DNS/Dovex as evidence of the next layer: regional labor infrastructure and Fair Food/WSR experimentation.

The most compelling narrative is:

In a conservative agricultural region facing labor shortages and federal rollback of worker protections, a small group of Washington tree-fruit employers are treating worker care as infrastructure. Their outlier status comes from combining ethics with business survival: certification, worker voice, legal migration, health access, safety systems, and shared labor pathways. (8/8)
