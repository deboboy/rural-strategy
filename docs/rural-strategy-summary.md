# Washington Rural Strategy

**Status:** Working draft · June 2026  
**Collaborators:** Frank Martinez, Leo S. Morales

---

## Executive summary

Washington’s rural and agricultural workers face preventable disease, long distances to care, irregular work schedules, language barriers, and— for many— fear of using services because of immigration status or employer retaliation. Clinics and public institutions exist across Eastern and Central Washington, but **access is episodic**: workers may receive a screening or outreach contact and then fall out of follow-up because transportation, paid time off, insurance navigation, and ongoing primary care are still missing.

This strategy connects four layers into one rural health operating model:

1. **Map where care and communities are** — rural health clinics, regional farmworker corridors, and trusted public hubs.
2. **Reach workers ethically through institutions** — not by hunting private messaging groups.
3. **Bring prevention to the workplace** — employer-hosted mobile screening as the front door.
4. **Close the loop with virtual care** — a Washington-owned telehealth operator for follow-up, chronic disease management, and navigation.
5. **Build toward systemic funding** — progressive employers now; state employer-payer models where mandates and coalitions can succeed later.

The near-term geography is **Eastern Washington**—Yakima Valley, Wenatchee/North Central, Grant/Adams, Tri-Cities, and allied corridors—where clinic density, farmworker concentration, and progressive employer exemplars overlap.

---

## The problem

Rural health disparities widened after the pandemic. Agricultural and food-processing workers are disproportionately affected by diabetes, hypertension, obesity-related risk, and vaccine-preventable illness. Many are hourly, low-wage, limited-English, or undocumented; employers hold most leverage over schedules and benefits.

Workers who do receive abnormal screening results often cannot complete follow-up because they lack:

- Paid time off for appointments
- Transportation to distant clinics
- A regular primary care relationship
- Trust that using services is safe

Employers, meanwhile, face labor shortages and rising H-2A costs. A small group of tree-fruit employers in Eastern Washington are already treating worker care as **retention infrastructure**—not charity—through certification, worker voice, ethical recruitment, and onsite health access.

## Core thesis: public hubs, private clusters

Farmworker communities are unlikely to appear as public social-media groups. The ethical outreach model is:

> **Private kinship, crew, housing, church, school, and messaging clusters—connected through trusted public institutions.**

Highest-centrality public nodes include Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic, the Migrant Education / ESD network, Northwest Justice Project Farmworker Unit, OIC of Washington, DOH temporary-worker housing data, Radio KDNA / El Proyecto Bienestar, CAFÉ in Wenatchee, and Centro Chinampa.

**Outreach insight:** identify the trusted public node that can ethically reach a private cluster—do not try to enumerate Signal, WhatsApp, or Telegram groups.

## Strategy stack

The rural strategy is not one program. It is a **stack of complementary interventions**:

### Layer 1 — Evidence and mapping

- Rural health clinic geodata and network summaries
- Farmworker social-graph research (public-source proxy only)
- Regional cluster and hub identification
- AI-assisted research and collaboration tools on this site

**Detailed brief:** [Farmworker social graph research](/farmworker-research.html)

---

### Layer 2 — Progressive employer partners

A small set of Eastern Washington agricultural employers have built above-compliance worker-care systems: EFI certification, worker-manager teams, ethical H-2A recruitment, onsite clinics, and measurable retention gains.

Core exemplars: **Stemilt Growers**, **Rainier Fruit / Zirkle**, **FirstFruits Farms**, **CMI Orchards / McDougall & Sons**. Regional infrastructure builders include **Stormy Mountain Labor Association** and emerging Fair Food / USDA-funded pathways.

These employers are practical pilot hosts—not because they represent the whole industry, but because they already treat worker care as operational infrastructure.

**Detailed brief:** [Progressive Eastern WA employers](/progressive-eastern-wa-employers.html)

---

### Layer 3 — Healthy Workplaces Eastern Washington (mobile screening pilot)

**Purpose:** deliver free, workplace-based cardiometabolic screening and vaccination with progressive employers co-funding and hosting events.

**Core model:** a community clinic or mobile health team brings pop-up screening to the jobsite—A1C, blood pressure, BMI, lipids where appropriate, health coaching, vaccines, and referrals. Employers provide paid time, hosting, promotion, and partial funding.

**Target workers:** food processing and agricultural workers, warehouse and manufacturing staff, hourly workers without robust benefits, and others facing distance and schedule barriers.

**Why it matters:** screening alone is episodic. This layer creates a trusted, employer-endorsed front door into the health system.

**Detailed brief:** [Healthy Workplaces East Washington pilot](/employer-payer-pilot.html)

---

### Layer 4 — Washington rural virtual care operator

**Purpose:** close the follow-up gap after screening and outreach.

Build and incubate a **Washington-owned rural virtual care operating company**—test in Eastern Washington, scale statewide after proving clinical quality, employer demand, reimbursement, and access outcomes.

**Operating model:**

1. **In-house platform startup** — brand, orchestration, employer relationships, CHW navigation, analytics.
2. **Local clinical anchors** — Confluence Health, Columbia Valley Community Health, WSU Extension/Promotores, employers, public health.
3. **Vendor/component partners** — video, EHR, eRx, labs, billing, credentialing, compliance (assemble, don’t rebuild everything).
4. **Employer/community access layer** — mobile screenings feed ongoing virtual primary care, chronic disease management, behavioral health, and specialty teleconsults.

**Offer framing:**

> A Washington-owned rural virtual care network that starts in Eastern Washington, combines mobile screenings with telehealth follow-up, and gives employers a practical way to improve worker health without becoming healthcare providers.

**Detailed brief:** [Washington rural virtual care operator](/wa-rural-telehealth-operator.html)

---

### Layer 5 — Systemic employer-payer policy (longer horizon)

Where voluntary employer programs are insufficient, research points to a **state-backed, enforceable employer-funded rural healthcare pool**—not employer goodwill.

The strongest policy lever is a state-mandated agricultural employer contribution fee funding coverage regardless of immigration status, with anti-retaliation protections, immigration-safe access, and a political coalition built before the mandate fight.

This layer is the systemic complement to voluntary progressive employers and pilot programs—not a substitute for near-term workplace and telehealth action.

**Detailed brief:** [Employer-payer healthcare rebuttal](/employer-payer-rebuttal.html)

## How the layers connect

```
Public hubs + clinic map
        ↓
Ethical outreach targeting
        ↓
Progressive employer hosts
        ↓
Mobile workplace screening (Healthy Workplaces)
        ↓
Virtual care follow-up (WA rural telehealth operator)
        ↓
Scale + policy (employer pool, grants, Medicaid, hospital community benefit)
```

Without Layer 4, workers with abnormal findings still depend on transportation, time off, and ad hoc navigation. Without Layer 2, workplace programs lack trusted hosts. Without Layer 1, outreach lacks geographic and institutional precision.

---

## Ethics and guardrails

- Use **public data only** for community mapping—no private group enumeration, no individual identification, no immigration status inference.
- Design programs so **workers are not the enforcement front line**.
- Provide **immigration-safe, culturally and linguistically appropriate** access channels.
- Treat this site as a **working prototype** for collaboration—not an official clinical directory or state resource.

---

## Near-term priorities

1. **Validate geography** — align clinic map, farmworker clusters, and employer exemplars in Eastern Washington.
2. **Design minimum viable pilot** — one to two progressive employers, mobile screening event, defined metrics (participation, abnormal-result follow-up rate, worker satisfaction).
3. **Stand up telehealth follow-up path** — even a thin virtual-care workflow before full platform incubation.
4. **Build employer consortium narrative** — co-funding, aggregate impact reporting, ESG/community investment story.
5. **Prepare funding package** — employer commitments, hospital community benefit, WA rural health grants, Medicaid managed care investment, platform seed funding.

## Bottom line

The Washington Rural Strategy is a **practical path from mapping to care**:

Meet workers where they are—through trusted institutions and progressive workplaces—screen for preventable disease on-site, and connect abnormal findings to ongoing virtual care under a Washington-owned operator. Prove the model in Eastern Washington with employers who already treat worker health as infrastructure, then scale through consortia, clinical partnerships, grants, and—where politically feasible—systemic employer funding.

The goal is not a single app or a single screening day. It is a **repeatable rural access operating system** for the workers who keep Washington’s agricultural economy moving.
