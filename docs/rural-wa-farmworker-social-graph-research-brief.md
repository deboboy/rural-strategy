# Research Brief: Rural Washington Farmworker Social-Group Network Map

**Status:** DRAFT  
**Date:** 2026-06-13  
**Mode:** research  
**Scope:** Public-source proxy mapping only; no private Signal/WhatsApp/Telegram groups, invite links, members, or individual identities are identified.

---

## Executive Summary

Rural Washington farmworker communities are not likely to appear as public social-media groups. The observable network is better modeled as **private kinship/crew/housing/school/church/messaging clusters connected through trusted public institutions**. The strongest public proxies for “centers of gravity” are:

1. **Yakima Valley** — the dominant hub: Yakima/Toppenish/Wapato/Sunnyside/Grandview/Granger/Moxee/Prosser.
2. **Grant/Adams** — Othello/Quincy/Royal City/Mattawa/Moses Lake/Ephrata.
3. **North Central/Wenatchee** — Wenatchee/East Wenatchee/Cashmere/Brewster/Bridgeport/Orondo/Manson/Chelan/Omak/Okanogan/Tonasket/Oroville.
4. **Tri-Cities/Benton-Franklin** — Pasco/Kennewick/Mesa/Benton City.
5. **Whatcom/Skagit** — Mount Vernon/Lynden and berry/dairy corridors.

The highest-centrality public nodes are **Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic**, **Migrant Education Program / ESD network**, **Northwest Justice Project Farmworker Unit**, **OIC of Washington**, **Office of Rural and Farmworker Housing / DOH temporary-worker-housing ecosystem**, **Radio KDNA / El Proyecto Bienestar**, **CAFÉ in Wenatchee**, and **Centro Chinampa / Fair Work Center**.

---

## Guardrails

- This brief maps **publicly observable institutions and geography**, not private groups.
- It intentionally does **not** identify private messaging groups, invite links, phone numbers beyond public organizational contact lines, members, immigration status, or individuals.
- Centrality scores below are **qualitative proxies** from public service reach, geographic coverage, and documented farmworker-facing work.

---

## Core Thesis

Farmworker social networks in rural Washington are probably organized around **private, trust-preserving channels**—family, hometown, crew, labor contractor, housing site, church, school, clinic, and messaging apps. Public institutions act as **bridges** between otherwise private clusters. Therefore, the best ethical network graph is a **hub-and-spoke model**: private clusters are represented as anonymized community nodes, while public hubs are mapped as connectors.

## Public Evidence Base

| Source | What it establishes |

## Regional Clusters

### 1. Yakima Valley Core — highest overall centrality

**Places:** Yakima, Toppenish, Wapato, Sunnyside, Grandview, Granger, Moxee, Zillah, Prosser.  
**Likely private-cluster anchors:** family networks, crew/labor contractor ties, housing sites, churches, schools, clinics, radio, worker centers.  
**Public hubs:** YVFWC, ESD 105/MEP, NJP Farmworker Unit, OIC, Radio KDNA/EPB, Centro Chinampa, ORFH/TWH.  
**Why central:** WSIPP found Yakima County had three times as many farmworkers as any other county; YVFWC originated here; multiple public hubs overlap.

### 2. Grant/Adams Ag Labor Belt — high structural centrality

**Places:** Othello, Quincy, Royal City, Mattawa, Moses Lake, Ephrata.  
**Public hubs:** OIC, ESD/MEP, ORFH/TWH, NJP outreach, community action, schools/churches.  
**DOH TWH signal:** Grant County had the most licensed temporary-worker-housing facilities in the parsed public sheet; Othello and Quincy were top cities by facility count.  
**Likely private-cluster anchors:** housing sites, crew bosses, schools, churches, WhatsApp/Signal groups by employer or hometown.

### 3. North Central / Wenatchee Tree-Fruit Corridor

**Places:** Wenatchee/East Wenatchee, Cashmere, Brewster, Bridgeport, Orondo, Manson, Chelan, Omak/Okanogan, Tonasket, Oroville, Pateros.  
**Public hubs:** CAFÉ, ESD 171/MEP, OIC Wenatchee, NJP outreach, YVFWC-adjacent clinics, ORFH/TWH.  
**DOH TWH signal:** Okanogan and Chelan rank high in licensed temporary-worker housing; Brewster, Bridgeport, Orondo, Wenatchee, Manson, Cashmere appear as facility clusters.  
**Likely private-cluster anchors:** orchard crews, seasonal migration chains, church ministries, school-family networks.

### 4. Tri-Cities / Benton-Franklin

**Places:** Pasco, Kennewick, Mesa, Benton City.  
**Public hubs:** ESD 123/MEP, OIC Pasco, NJP outreach, YVFWC Tri-Cities clinics, ORFH/TWH, community action/legal aid.  
**DOH TWH signal:** Benton and Franklin counties have substantial licensed housing footprints; Pasco, Mesa, Benton City, and Kennewick appear in facility data.  
**Likely private-cluster anchors:** packing/field crews, housing sites, churches, school-family networks, Spanish-language media.

### 5. Whatcom/Skagit Berry-Dairy Corridor

**Places:** Mount Vernon, Lynden, surrounding rural areas.  
**Public hubs:** OIC Mt. Vernon, ESD 189/MEP, legal aid/community action, housing/worker-service networks.  
**DOH TWH signal:** Whatcom and Skagit appear in the public housing dataset.  
**Likely private-cluster anchors:** berry/dairy crews, family networks, churches, schools, migrant education.

## Messaging-App Hypothesis

Private messaging groups are likely to form around:

1. **Family/hometown networks** — trust, remittances, mutual aid, travel, documents.
2. **Employer/crew/labor-contractor networks** — shift schedules, rides, pay issues.
3. **Housing-site networks** — immediate needs, repairs, safety, shared transportation.
4. **School/MEP parent networks** — children, transportation, forms, health appointments.
5. **Church/faith/community ministry networks** — food, clothing, emergency aid.
6. **Clinic/CHW/promotora networks** — appointments, health education, insurance, prescriptions.
7. **Legal/worker-center networks** — rights, wage theft, retaliation, safety, organizing.
8. **Spanish-language media/radio networks** — public announcements, events, resource drives.

The clue is not “find the Telegram group”; it is “identify the trusted public node that can ethically reach the private cluster.”

## Recommended Next Research Steps

1. **Validate with field interviews** using community-led partners, not scraping/private-group discovery.
2. **Build a geocoded public-hub layer** from clinics, MEP offices, OIC sites, legal aid outreach, churches, food banks, community action agencies, and TWH sites.
3. **Add seasonal flow modeling** by crop calendar: cherries/apples/hops/berries/dairy/packing.
4. **Interview CHWs/promotoras, MEP recruiters, legal aid outreach workers, clergy, and housing/site staff** under privacy-preserving protocols.
5. **Use graph metrics cautiously:** degree = service reach; betweenness = bridge role; eigenvector = overlap with other high-trust hubs.
6. **Avoid exposing private groups or individuals**; report clusters at town/county/service-hub level unless explicit consent is obtained.

---

## Key Risks

- Public data overrepresents people who interact with services and underrepresents more hidden workers.
- Identifying “centers” can create surveillance or enforcement risk if misused.
- County-level data hides intra-county microclusters.
- Seasonal migration means centrality shifts by crop and month.
- Private messaging groups are intentionally opaque for safety reasons; attempting to enumerate them would be unethical and potentially harmful.
