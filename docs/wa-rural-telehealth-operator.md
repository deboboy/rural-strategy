# Washington rural virtual care operator

## Overview

Build a Washington-owned rural virtual care operating company, test it in Eastern Washington, and scale it statewide after proving clinical quality, employer demand, reimbursement, and rural access outcomes.

# BEM Telehealth Operator Incubation Strategy

## Executive recommendation

Build and incubate the **white-label telehealth operator in-house** as a Washington-first platform startup.

The strategy should shift from “partner with an existing telehealth operator” to:

> **Create a Washington-branded rural virtual care operating company, test it in Eastern Washington, and scale it statewide after proving clinical quality, employer demand, reimbursement, and rural access outcomes.**

The in-house operator should own the patient relationship, brand, data layer, care navigation, employer reporting, and platform economics. It should **not** build every regulated component from scratch. Instead, it should assemble and integrate best-in-class modules for video, EHR/documentation, eRx, labs, payments, compliance, revenue cycle, and analytics.

Recommended operating model:

1. **In-house platform startup** — owns the telehealth brand, product, orchestration, employer relationships, CHW navigation layer, and Washington market proof.
2. **Local clinical anchors** — Confluence Health, Columbia Valley Community Health, WSU Extension/Promotores, employers, and public health partners provide local trust, referral pathways, and pilot access.
3. **Vendor/component partners** — use external infrastructure for regulated or capital-intensive pieces: video, EHR, eRx, labs, billing, credentialing support, compliance tooling, and specialty networks where needed.
4. **Employer/community access layer** — mobile workplace screenings become the front door into ongoing virtual care, chronic disease management, behavioral health, and specialty teleconsults.

The first offer should be framed as:

> **A Washington-owned rural virtual care network that starts in Eastern Washington, combines mobile screenings with telehealth follow-up, and gives employers a practical way to improve worker health without becoming healthcare providers.**

---

## Why this is the right next dimension

The previous two pilots solve different parts of the same access problem:

- **Cascaded Care / Chelan-Douglas:** rural specialty access, especially retinal screening, CHW training, and low-bandwidth telemedicine.
- **Healthy Workplaces Eastern Washington:** employer-funded mobile cardiometabolic screening and vaccination.
- **Telehealth layer:** connects the front-door screening event to ongoing care, follow-up, prescriptions, labs, behavioral health, and specialist referrals.

Without telehealth, the model risks becoming episodic: workers get screened, some receive abnormal findings, but follow-up still depends on them finding transportation, time, insurance navigation, and a clinician. The telehealth operator closes that loop.

## Target operating model

### Brand / market position

Use a Washington-specific brand or sub-brand, for example:

- **Washington Rural Virtual Care Network**
- **Healthy Workplaces Virtual Care Network**
- **Eastern Washington Care Connect**

The brand should feel local and public-benefit oriented, not like a generic national telehealth app.

### Core promise

> Free or low-cost access to screening, virtual care, follow-up, and navigation for rural and hourly Washington workers — hosted by employers, supported by local clinics, and powered by the in-house virtual care operator.

### Service lines

Start with four telehealth service lines:

1. **Virtual primary and urgent care**
   - Common acute issues
   - Medication questions
   - Follow-up after abnormal screening results
   - Referral guidance

2. **Cardiometabolic care management**
   - Diabetes / prediabetes follow-up
   - Hypertension monitoring
   - Lipid management
   - Weight and nutrition coaching
   - Lab ordering and medication titration where clinically appropriate

3. **Behavioral health**
   - Anxiety, depression, stress, substance-use screening
   - Short-term counseling
   - Warm handoffs to local behavioral health partners
   - Employer-safe, confidential access for workers

4. **Specialty teleconsults**
   - Retina / diabetic retinopathy
   - Dermatology
   - Cardiology
   - Women’s health
   - Occupational health questions

## Incubation structure

### 1. In-house telehealth operator

The new company should become the **operating system** for rural virtual care in Washington.

It should own:

- The Washington-facing brand
- Patient relationship and consent layer
- Employer relationships and reporting
- CHW/Promotores navigation console
- Care orchestration workflows
- Screening-to-virtual-care handoff
- De-identified outcomes dashboard
- Product roadmap and platform economics
- Quality measurement and payer/employer proof points

It should not initially own every regulated component. The company should incubate the **coordination layer, product layer, and market proof**, then buy or license the most regulated infrastructure.

Core platform modules to build or integrate:

| Module | Build / buy recommendation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Patient portal / app | Build or white-label | Must support low-bandwidth, mobile-first, bilingual access |
| Clinician portal | Buy/integrate first | Needs scheduling, documentation, video/chat, tasking |
| Video visits | Buy | Use HIPAA-compliant video infrastructure |
| Async visits / messaging | Buy or integrate | Critical for rural workers with unpredictable schedules |
| EHR-lite documentation | Build carefully, integrate later | Start with structured visit notes, then FHIR/HL7 export |
| ePrescribing | Buy via vendor | Requires Surescripts/DEA workflow compliance |
| eLabs | Buy/integrate | Needed for A1C follow-up, lipids, kidney function, etc. |
| Revenue cycle | Partner initially | Medicaid/commercial billing can be complex |
| Credentialing/licensing | Partner + internal ops | Need WA-licensed clinicians and medical director |
| Compliance/HIPAA | Internal owner + external counsel/tooling | Must own policies, BAAs, incident response |
| Analytics/reporting | Build | This is a core differentiator |
| AI workflow support | Build carefully | Use for triage, documentation support, navigation, not autonomous diagnosis |

### 2. Local clinical anchor and proof market

**Primary target:** Chelan/Douglas County

Potential anchors:

- **Confluence Health** — regional clinical credibility, hospital/clinic infrastructure, specialty access, telehealth capacity.
- **Columbia Valley Community Health** — FQHC reach into underserved and agricultural worker populations.
- **Chelan-Douglas Health District** — public health coordination and community trust.
- **WSU Extension Chelan/Douglas / Promotores de Salud** — CHW recruitment, training, and community navigation.
- **Local employers** — tree fruit, food processing, logistics, public-sector employers, schools, and regional manufacturers.

The local anchor should own or co-own:

- Clinical protocols
- Local referral pathways
- Community trust
- Public health alignment
- Worker-facing messaging
- Follow-up expectations

### 3. Startup governance

The incubated operator should be structured as a proper healthcare startup, not just a software product.

Minimum governance needs:

- Washington-licensed medical director
- Clinical advisory board with rural primary care, FQHC, behavioral health, and specialty representation
- HIPAA/security officer
- Compliance counsel
- Revenue cycle lead
- Clinical operations lead
- Product/engineering lead
- Employer partnerships lead
- CHW/navigation lead

Likely legal structure should be reviewed with healthcare counsel:

- Professional entity for clinical services where required
- Management services organization / platform company for non-clinical operations where appropriate
- Clear separation between clinical decision-making and business operations
- BAAs, payer contracts, employer agreements, and data-use agreements

### 4. Statewide expansion layer

After Eastern Washington proof, expand through:

- WA State Office of Rural Health
- WA Department of Health telehealth resources
- WA Health Care Authority / Medicaid managed care partners
- Rural hospitals and critical access hospitals
- FQHCs
- Employer consortia
- Labor unions / workforce associations
- Tribal health partners where appropriate

The expansion thesis should be:

> Prove the model in Chelan/Douglas, productize the workflows, then sell the Washington rural virtual care operating system to other counties, employers, clinics, and payers.

## Component partner / acquisition shortlist

The incubated company should still evaluate outside partners. The question changes from “who operates the telehealth business for us?” to “which components should we buy, license, or acquire as we build the operator?”

| Partner type | Candidate examples | Role in incubation | Concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| White-label virtual care infrastructure | **Wheel** | Possible early operating partner, benchmark, or later acquisition target | Could compete with in-house ownership goals |
| Enterprise telehealth platform | **Amwell** | Video/EHR-integrated platform component or benchmark | May be more enterprise/provider-system oriented than startup-friendly |
| Employer/member virtual care platform | **Included Health / Doctor On Demand** | Behavioral health, navigation, employer benefit benchmark | May be less white-label / less locally branded |
| Large national telehealth operator | **Teladoc Health** | Specialty access, benchmark, possible later partnership | Brand dilution and less local control |
| Specialty telehealth operator | **Avel eCare** | Emergency/urgent telemedicine layer for rural hospitals | Not a full primary care / chronic care platform by itself |
| Remote exam / diagnostic complement | **TytoCare** | Remote physical exam devices for homes, schools, worksites | Hardware cost and workflow integration needed |
| eRx / pharmacy routing | Surescripts-connected vendor | Required regulated component | Must verify WA workflow and formulary support |
| Labs / point-of-care integration | Lab and POC vendors | A1C, lipids, metabolic follow-up | Needs consent, ordering, and result-routing workflows |
| Compliance / security tooling | HIPAA/security vendors | Policies, risk assessments, BAAs, audit trails | Must be owned internally, not outsourced |

**Recommended posture:** incubate the operating company and core orchestration layer in-house. Use vendors for regulated components until the startup has enough volume, capital, and compliance maturity to bring more functions inside.

## Eastern Washington incubation pilot design

### Pilot name

**Healthy Workplaces + Virtual Care: Eastern Washington Launch**

### Duration

6–9 months

### Geography

Start with:

- Chelan County
- Douglas County
- Wenatchee / East Wenatchee
- Cashmere, Leavenworth, Entiat, Brewster, Bridgeport, Pateros
- Optional Phase 1B: Grant/Adams or Yakima if employer demand is strong

### Pilot sites

Start with 4–6 sites:

1. Confluence Health clinic or mobile event site
2. Columbia Valley Community Health site
3. Employer worksite in tree fruit / food processing
4. Employer worksite in logistics / warehousing
5. Community event through WSU Extension / Promotores
6. Optional farmworker housing or church/community center site

### Participants

Target:

- 500–1,000 workers screened
- 200–400 virtual care encounters
- 50–100 chronic care follow-up enrollments
- 25–40 behavioral health referrals or consults
- 20–30 specialty teleconsults, including retina where clinically indicated

### Event-to-virtual-care workflow

1. **Employer hosts screening event**
   - Paid time for workers
   - Private screening space
   - Internal promotion
   - No access to individual health data

2. **Mobile screening team performs intake**
   - A1C
   - Blood pressure
   - BMI / waist circumference
   - Optional lipid panel
   - Vaccination eligibility
   - Behavioral health / social needs screening if appropriate

3. **Risk stratification**
   - Green: preventive education
   - Yellow: telehealth follow-up within 14–30 days
   - Red: same-day clinical guidance, local urgent care/ED referral if needed

4. **Telehealth follow-up**
   - Virtual primary care visit
   - Lab ordering
   - ePrescribing
   - Chronic disease plan
   - Behavioral health appointment
   - Specialty consult if needed

5. **CHW navigation**
   - Appointment reminders
   - Insurance/Medicaid navigation
   - Transportation support
   - Language access
   - Follow-up completion tracking

6. **Employer reporting**
   - De-identified aggregate report only
   - Participation
   - Common risk categories
   - Follow-up completion
   - Worker satisfaction
   - Program recommendations

## Business model

### Revenue / funding sources

1. **Employer sponsorship**
   - Employers pay for events, supplies, transportation, and/or per-worker screening.
   - Employers also provide paid time and space.

2. **In-house platform / subscription revenue**
   - Per-employer platform fee, per-member-per-month, or per-virtual-visit fee.
   - Payer or clinic contracts once the operator proves outcomes.

3. **Clinical billing**
   - Medicaid
   - Commercial insurance
   - Medicare where applicable
   - Self-pay / sliding scale for uninsured workers

4. **Hospital / clinic community benefit**
   - Confluence Health or regional hospitals may fund access expansion.

5. **Public health and rural health grants**
   - WA Office of Rural Health
   - Federal Office for the Advancement of Telehealth
   - HRSA rural health network funding
   - Medicaid managed care community investment funds

6. **Specialty care contracts**
   - Retina, behavioral health, cardiology, dermatology, occupational health

### Suggested employer packages

| Package | Includes | Suggested price |
|---|---|---:|
| Screening-only | Mobile screening event + aggregate report | $5,000–$10,000 per event |
| Screening + virtual care access | Screening + 3–6 months of worker telehealth access | $10,000–$25,000 per employer |
| Full workplace health program | Screening + virtual primary care + chronic care + behavioral health navigation | $25,000–$50,000 per employer |
| Regional consortium | Multiple employers in one county or industry cluster | Custom pooled model |

## Product, vendor, and regulatory diligence questions

Before selecting vendors or component partners, require answers to:

1. Can you support **Washington Medicaid**, Medicare, and commercial billing?
2. Do you have clinicians licensed in Washington with availability for rural adult primary care?
3. Can the platform be fully **white-labeled** under a Washington/Eastern Washington brand?
4. Can you support Spanish-language care and interpreter workflows?
5. Can you integrate with local clinic workflows or at least export structured visit notes?
6. Do you support ePrescribing, eLabs, and referral routing?
7. Can you support async visits, not just live video?
8. Do you have behavioral health clinicians?
9. Do you have specialty access for retina, cardiology, dermatology, and women’s health?
10. Can you operate in low-bandwidth environments with phone-first fallback?
11. Who owns the patient relationship and data?
12. What compliance obligations remain with the local clinical anchor?
13. What reporting is available for de-identified employer/community impact?
14. Can the model support CHW/Promotores navigation and warm handoffs?
15. What are the minimum volume commitments and implementation timeline?

## Investor / pilot partner pitch for the in-house operator

The pitch should not be “help us build a telehealth app.”

It should be:

> We are building a Washington-owned rural virtual care operating company that starts in Eastern Washington with employer-funded demand, local clinic partnerships, CHW navigation, and mobile screening events. We are incubating the platform in-house so Washington keeps the patient relationship, data layer, care navigation, and platform economics. Together, we create a repeatable statewide access model that gives rural workers a front door to care and gives employers a measurable health benefit without exposing them to individual health data.

### Why this should win

- Washington-first brand and governance
- Rural access wedge with employer-funded demand
- Hybrid mobile + virtual care model, not just app-based telehealth
- CHW/Promotores navigation layer tailored to agricultural and hourly workers
- Potential Medicaid, employer, hospital community benefit, and grant revenue
- Scalable template for other rural counties and states
- Strong community trust through local partners
- Lower acquisition cost because workers are reached through worksites, clinics, and CHWs
- Platform asset that can become the operating layer for rural virtual care

## Incubation roadmap

### 0–90 days: formation and validation

- Incorporate the platform startup and define clinical/business legal structure with healthcare counsel.
- Recruit Washington-licensed medical director and clinical advisory board.
- Select initial vendor stack for video, documentation, eRx, labs, payments, security, and analytics.
- Secure LOIs from 3–5 Eastern Washington employers.
- Secure LOIs from Confluence Health, Columbia Valley Community Health, and WSU Extension/Promotores.
- Define first 90-day pilot: 2 screening events, 1 weekly virtual care block, 1 CHW navigator, and de-identified reporting.
- Draft compliance package: HIPAA policies, BAAs, incident response, consent, data-use agreements, clinical escalation protocols.

### 3–6 months: MVP and first pilot

- Launch patient-facing mobile/web portal with low-bandwidth mode and bilingual support.
- Launch clinician workflow for scheduled, urgent, and async visits.
- Connect screening intake to virtual care referrals.
- Run first employer screening events.
- Track virtual visits, follow-up completion, abnormal-result closure, and employer participation.
- Begin Medicaid/commercial billing feasibility work.
- Build employer dashboard with de-identified aggregate data only.

### 6–12 months: operating proof

- Expand to 4–6 sites.
- Add chronic disease management for diabetes, hypertension, and lipids.
- Add behavioral health consults or referral pathway.
- Add retina/dermatology/cardiology teleconsult pilots.
- Formalize payer strategy with WA Medicaid managed care and commercial employers.
- Publish first de-identified impact report.
- Raise pre-seed/seed funding or secure strategic capital from health system/employer consortium.

### 12–18 months: statewide scale

- Productize the operating playbook.
- Expand to Columbia Basin/Tri-Cities and Yakima Valley.
- Add rural hospital and FQHC contracts.
- Build specialty consult pools.
- Standardize employer packages.
- Pursue WA Office of Rural Health, HCA, HRSA, and philanthropic support.
- Prepare for multi-state expansion only after Washington unit economics and clinical quality are proven.
