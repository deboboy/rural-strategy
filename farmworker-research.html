Farmworker social-group network research

A public-source proxy map of how rural Washington farmworker communities likely connect through trusted institutions—not private messaging groups. Draft research brief, June 2026.

Read the full research brief on GitHub (Markdown) — includes evidence tables, source URLs, and detailed regional notes.

Executive summary

Farmworker communities in rural Washington are unlikely to appear as public social-media groups. The better model is private kinship, crew, housing, school, church, and messaging clusters linked through trusted public institutions.

Five major geographic corridors stand out:

  1. Yakima Valley — Yakima, Toppenish, Wapato, Sunnyside, Grandview, Granger, Moxee, Prosser
  2. Grant/Adams — Othello, Quincy, Royal City, Mattawa, Moses Lake, Ephrata
  3. North Central / Wenatchee — Wenatchee, Cashmere, Brewster, Bridgeport, Omak, Chelan
  4. Tri-Cities / Benton-Franklin — Pasco, Kennewick, Mesa, Benton City
  5. Whatcom / Skagit — Mount Vernon, Lynden, berry and dairy corridors

Highest-centrality public nodes include Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic, the Migrant Education / ESD network, Northwest Justice Project Farmworker Unit, OIC of Washington, ORFH / DOH temporary-worker housing data, Radio KDNA / El Proyecto Bienestar, CAFÉ in Wenatchee, and Centro Chinampa.

Ethics & guardrails

This research maps publicly observable institutions and geography only. It does not identify private Signal, WhatsApp, or Telegram groups, invite links, members, immigration status, or individuals. Centrality scores are qualitative proxies from public service reach—not surveillance data.

Core thesis

Networks are organized around private, trust-preserving channels—family, crew, contractor, housing site, church, school, clinic, and messaging apps. Public institutions act as bridges between otherwise private clusters. The ethical graph is hub-and-spoke: anonymized community nodes connected through mapped public hubs.

Tier 1 public hubs

  • Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic — broadest health and family-support footprint; farmworker-origin mission
  • Migrant Education / ESD network — bridges children, parents, schools, and services across crop regions
  • Northwest Justice Project Farmworker Unit — legal aid and camp/housing outreach statewide
  • OIC of Washington — workforce, education, and community support across Eastern WA corridors
  • ORFH / DOH temporary-worker housing — structural signal for where worker populations concentrate (Grant, Okanogan, Yakima lead in licensed facilities)
  • Radio KDNA / El Proyecto Bienestar — Spanish-language radio and community health in the Yakima Valley
  • CAFÉ — Wenatchee-based Latino-serving resources in the tree-fruit corridor
  • Centro Chinampa / Fair Work Center — organizing and resource hub in Yakima

Regional clusters (brief)

  • Yakima Valley core — highest overall centrality; WSIPP found Yakima County had 3× more farmworkers than any other county
  • Grant/Adams belt — Grant County leads in licensed temporary-worker housing; Othello and Quincy are top facility cities
  • North Central / Wenatchee — orchard crews, seasonal migration; Okanogan and Chelan rank high in housing data
  • Tri-Cities — packing and field crews; ESD 123 and OIC Pasco among key bridges
  • Whatcom / Skagit — berry and dairy crews; OIC Mt. Vernon and ESD 189

Messaging-app hypothesis

Private groups likely form around family/hometown ties, employer/crew networks, housing sites, school/MEP parents, churches, clinic/promotora networks, legal/worker centers, and Spanish-language media. The outreach insight is not “find the Telegram group”—it is identify the trusted public node that can ethically reach the private cluster.

Recommended next steps

  • Validate with field interviews through community-led partners
  • Build a geocoded public-hub layer (clinics, MEP, OIC, legal aid, TWH sites)
  • Model seasonal flows by crop calendar
  • Interview CHWs, MEP recruiters, outreach workers, and clergy under privacy-preserving protocols
  • Report at town/county/hub level—avoid exposing private groups or individuals

Key risks

  • Public data overrepresents people who use services and underrepresents hidden workers
  • Identifying “centers” can create surveillance risk if misused
  • County-level data hides microclusters; centrality shifts by season and crop
